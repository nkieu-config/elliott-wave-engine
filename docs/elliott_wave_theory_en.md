## Page 13

'Odd' and 'Even' numbering

A note about determining whether a wave is a Push or Pull: look at the number — whether it is odd or even. If it is odd, it is definitely a Push, but if it is even, it is definitely a Pull.

The reason this rule works is that, when any trend begins, it always starts with 1, which is an odd number. As a result, odd-numbered waves are automatically defined as 'waves following the main trend' or Push-Waves. And even-numbered waves are automatically defined as 'waves countering the main trend' or Pull-Waves.

So remember:

| Odd number = wave following the main trend (Push)
| Even number = wave countering the main trend (Pull)

Push-Wave = 1 3 5 7 9 11 13 ....
Pull-Wave = 2 4 6 8 10 12 ....

Main Rules of the 5-Wave Structure [ TREND ]

Although the 5 Segments can connect in any way, there are rules about the proportions of each wave. They must
connect in a way that meets these conditions in order to be called a 5-Wave Trend type. And if a structure set being
analyzed does not meet the main rules of the 5-Wave Trend type, then it will be a different pattern within the theory.
The main rules are as follows:

1. Must have 5 consecutive waves with a clear main trend
2. Waves s1, s3, and s5 must travel in the same direction as the main trend (Push Wave)
3. Waves s2 and s4 are corrective waves or counter the main trend (Pull Wave)
4. Wave s3 must always be longer than wave s2
5. Wave s3, when compared with waves s1 and s5, must not be the shortest (but does not need to be the longest)
6. Wave s5 can be shorter or longer than wave s4. But if shorter, wave s5 must have a length of at least 38.2%
   of wave s4 in order to be considered wave s5, and it will be called s5-Shorter

## Page 30

Trend-Line Concept

Another equally important matter is 'trend lines'. Many people may consider it secondary and unimportant, but nonetheless, it can help us count waves more easily.

The trend lines in the 5-Wave Trend pattern consist of three trend lines as follows:

1. Trend line 0-s2 (drawn from the start of s1 to the end of s2)
2. Trend line s1-s3 (drawn from the end of s1 to the end of s3)
3. Trend line s2-s4 (drawn from the end of s2 to the end of s4)

The three trend lines have different uses. For example, 0-s2 is used to check whether s3 has begun, s1-s3 is used to check support and resistance, and s2-s4 is used to check whether the entire structure is finished.

Trend-Line [ O-s2 ]

The O-s2 trend line is very useful in checking whether s2 has truly ended and whether s3 has begun. The conditions are as follows:

- When s2 ends at the point where we think it really ended, the price afterward must move away from it; it should not stay touching the O-s2 trend line.

Start by drawing the trend line from the start of 1 to the point where we think 2 has ended.
If price moves away like this, it means s2 has truly ended.
But if price still comes down and stays on the O-s2 trend line, it means s2 may not be finished yet.

Trend-Line [ s2-s4 ]

The s2-s4 trend line is the most useful of all three trend lines. It can check the completion of the entire pattern and is also a trend line that makes wave counting much easier. The conditions are as follows:

Main Rules of the 5-Wave Structure [ Sideway ]

Apart from the 5-Wave being a Trend, it can also be a Sideway, and remember that the rules are completely unrelated even though both have 5 waves. The main rules of the 5-Wave Sideway type are as follows:

1. Must have 5 consecutive waves, no more and no less, but unlike the Trend type, it moves sideways. It does not have a clear directional trend.

2. Even though it moves sideways, it can have an upward or downward slant, without breaking the structural characteristics of being a Sideway type.

The main rules are only these, because the sub-patterns within this type have their own rules. The sub-patterns are divided into 3 types only: Contract (squeezing in), Balance (balanced), Expand (broadening out).

Introduction to the wave linking principle [ Link-Wave ]

In addition to creating 5-Wave and 3-Wave types of waves, they can also create higher-degree waves
again. If we tried to name them all, it would take a very long time to study the theory. Therefore in this section, we will
talk about the wave-linking principle, which the analysis system calls Link-Wave, and we use the symbol ' + ' to denote it.

Structures can only be linked through the 3-Wave (any) and 5-Wave (Sideway only) families. Every time
a wave linkage occurs with a ' + ' wave, this wave is by nature a Pull-Wave, or a wave countering the main trend.
Wave linking can be divided into two main types as follows:

1. +T (Trend Link-Wave) - a linking that makes the structure form a clear, continuous trend
2. +S (Sideway Link-Wave) - a linking that makes the structure form a continuous sideways move

The rules and conditions of +T and +S linking are clearly different and must be understood separately. Do not
get them confused.

## Page 130

Choosing the starting point for counting waves with Important High/Low

The simplest way to choose the starting point for counting waves is to choose by Important High or Important Low.
Their definition is the significant high or significant low, but more precisely it must be..

'It is a point where price reverses, due to the structure rising or falling in one direction only'

The selection principle is simple: select High, Low points in a large-scale price chart, such as Month, Week, or Day, that
have passed for some period and price has not gone up or down to break that High or Low. This indicates that those Highs/Lows
may be points where price has finished some structural pattern and started a new structure set from that point.

## Page 131

Clipping the chart to count waves

The method of clipping the chart is to clip out all the past structure, ignore the past structure, and start counting waves immediately at a point that is not an Important High or Low. This method is strongly not recommended for those who are just starting to study wave theory because it will cause many counting errors. So this method should be used only after you are skilled.

For chart clipping, the correct way is that you must clip the chart at the part where the pattern has truly ended — not clip the chart in the middle of a section where the pattern is forming. If you do that, you cannot count waves at all; no matter how you count, it will be wrong. The hard part is not the counting but choosing the correct counting point. So we do not recommend this for beginners.

## Page 132

Analyzing from the big picture down to the small picture
This kind of analysis is called Top-Down Analysis. It means analyzing from the big picture down to the small picture. Beginners must always remember 'always count waves from big picture down to small picture'. That is, you cannot start counting the chart in a small timeframe immediately. You must look at the entire overview, otherwise it will confuse you about the market position, and you will not be able to count waves at all.
The reason we must count the big-picture waves first is that, in the small-timeframe structure, Segments are continuously created and connected, becoming very large structures over a long time. So when we analyze the big picture first, we know what structure those tiny waves are running in: whether they are in a Push or Pull state. And it makes wave counting much easier, because starting to count at a small timeframe is complex, while the big picture is much simpler and provides complete information at every step.
